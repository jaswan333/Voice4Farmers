Tomato - Early Blight (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are there brown rings on older leaves?

Why are leaves turning yellow and falling early?

Symptoms:

Brown, circular spots with concentric rings on older leaves

Yellow halo around spots, leaf turns yellow and drops early

Reduced leaves, small fruits, poor yield

Cause:
Fungal infection caused by Alternaria solani in warm, humid weather.

Solution:

Spray Mancozeb or Chlorothalonil 0.2–0.3% starting at first spot, repeat 2–3 times at 10–14‑day intervals

Use resistant or tolerant varieties if available

Prevention:

Avoid excess nitrogen and dense planting

Avoid overhead irrigation, keep leaves dry

Remove infected leaves and crop residues after harvest

Tomato - Late Blight (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves turning brown and wet?

Why are plant and fruits in the field rotting?

Symptoms:

Dark green, water‑soaked spots on leaves, quickly turning brown

White fungal growth seen on leaf undersides in cool, wet weather

Stems and fruits get brown, soft spots, rotting

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Phytophthora infestans in cool, wet conditions.

Solution:

Use late‑blight‑tolerant varieties, avoid continuous tomato

Spray Metalaxyl‑M + Mancozeb 0.2–0.3% at first signs, repeat 2–3 times at 7–10‑day intervals

Prevention:

Avoid dense planting and overhead irrigation

Keep field drainage and spacing good

Remove infected plants and debris, avoid night irrigation

Tomato - Bacterial Wilt (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are plants suddenly wilting even with water?

Why are stems brown inside when cut?

Symptoms:

One or more branches wilt suddenly, then whole plant

Leaves stay green, but stem shows brown inside

Plant collapses, root may rot

Cause:
Bacterial infection caused by Ralstonia solanacearum entering through roots and wounds.

Solution:

Remove and destroy infected plants with surrounding soil

Treat seedlings with hot water or approved bactericides before planting

Avoid planting in that plot immediately for next season

Prevention:

Rotate tomato with non‑solanaceous crops

Avoid heavy clay, water‑logged soil

Use disease‑free, certified seedlings

Tomato - Septoria Leaf Spot (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves getting many small black spots?

Why are leaves dying from bottom?

Symptoms:

Small round spots with dark margin and gray center on lower leaves

Tiny black dots (pycnidia) visible in the center of spots

Leaves yellow and drop early from bottom to top

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Septoria lycopersici in humid conditions.

Solution:

Spray Mancozeb or Chlorothalonil 0.2–0.3% when spots appear, repeat 2–3 times at 10‑day intervals

Remove heavily infected leaves

Prevention:

Avoid overhead irrigation on leaves

Keep spacing open for air movement

Rotate crop and remove infected plant material

Tomato - Tomato Leaf Curl Virus (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves curling and plants short?

Why are plants bushy and fruits poor?

Symptoms:

Leaves curl upward, narrow, twisted, yellow‑mottled

Plants short, bushy, with reduced flowering and fruiting

Fruits small, few, and poor quality

Cause:
Viral disease spread by whiteflies that feed on leaves.

Solution:

Remove and destroy infected plants early

Use leaf‑curl‑resistant varieties where available

Control whiteflies with approved insecticides

Prevention:

Use virus‑free, healthy seedlings

Control whiteflies early with neonicotinoid/Imidacloprid sprays

Use yellow sticky traps and reduce solanaceous weeds

Tomato - Nitrogen Deficiency (Nutrient Deficiency)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are older leaves turning yellow?

Why is crop thin and slow‑growing?

Symptoms:

Older leaves turn pale green to yellow, starting from tip

Plants look thin, less leaves and weak growth

Small, poor‑quality fruits

Cause:
Low nitrogen in soil or poor fertilizer management.

Solution:

Top‑dress with urea 15–25 kg/ha split at early vegetative and fruit‑setting stage

Combine with organic manure or compost

Prevention:

Follow balanced NPK recommendations for tomato

Avoid only nitrogen‑rich practice without potassium and micro‑nutrients

Avoid late, heavy nitrogen doses

Tomato - Calcium Deficiency – Blossom End Rot (Nutrient Deficiency)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are fruits rotting at the blossom end?

Why are fruits developing dark, sunken spots?

Symptoms:

Dark, sunken, leathery spot at the bottom of the fruit

Spot may enlarge, fruit quality poor

Plant may look healthy, but many fruits affected

Cause:
Low calcium or uneven water supply during fruiting stage.

Solution:

Spray foliar calcium 0.3–0.5% twice a week during fruiting, as per label

Keep soil moisture even through drip or regular irrigation

Prevention:

Maintain uniform irrigation, avoid dry‑wet shock

Avoid excess nitrogen that reduces calcium uptake

Use balanced fertilizers with micro‑nutrients if soil is poor

Tomato - Whitefly (Pest)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves sticky and black?

Why are plants stunted and yellowing?

Symptoms:

Small white insects seen flying when leaves are disturbed

Leaves sticky from honeydew, with black sooty mould

Plants weak, small fruits, may show virus damage

Cause:
Insect sucking sap and spreading viruses like leaf‑curl and mosaic.

Solution:

Spray Imidacloprid or Acetamiprid as per label dose at first sight

Use yellow sticky traps to reduce adult population

Prevention:

Avoid continuous solanaceous crops in same field

Maintain spacing and avoid heavy nitrogen

Use reflective mulch or nets where possible

Tomato - Thrips (Pest)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves silvery and twisted?

Why are flowers dropping and fruits bad?

Symptoms:

Leaves silvered, distorted, with small streaks or scratches

Flowers may drop, fruits small, rough, or marbled

Tiny, slender insects on leaf undersides

Cause:
Thrips feeding on leaves and flowers, often in hot, dry conditions.

Solution:

Spray Spinosad or Imidacloprid/Thiamethoxam as per label at early signs

Spray in early morning or late evening

Prevention:

Avoid dense, overcrowded planting

Reduce high nitrogen, keep canopy airy

Use blue or yellow sticky traps and neem‑based spray if possible
