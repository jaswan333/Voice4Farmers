=== CROP: Groundnut ===

Groundnut - Early Leaf Spot (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are small brown spots forming on older leaves?

Why are leaves drying from bottom upwards?

Symptoms:

Small brown or dark brown circular spots on older leaves

Spots may join, leaves turn yellow and dry early

Reduced leaf area, fewer pods and low yield

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Cercospora arachidicola in humid, wet conditions.

Solution:

Spray Mancozeb or Copper‑based fungicides 0.2–0.3% at early spots, repeat 2–3 times at 10‑day intervals

Use resistant or tolerant varieties where available

Prevention:

Avoid dense planting and excess nitrogen

Keep field well‑drained, avoid waterlogging

Rotate with non‑legume crops and remove infected leaves

Groundnut - Late Leaf Spot (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves getting dark brown patches before drying?

Why are leaves showing large dark lesions?

Symptoms:

Larger dark brown to black lesions on leaves, often with faint border

Lesions may join, leaves turn yellow and fall early

Reduced photosynthesis, fewer filled pods

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Phaeoisariopsis personata in warm, humid weather.

Solution:

Use late‑leaf‑spot‑tolerant varieties if available

Spray Mancozeb or broad‑spectrum fungicides 0.2–0.3% at first sign, repeat 2–3 times at 10‑day intervals

Prevention:

Maintain proper spacing and avoid overcrowding

Avoid heavy, continuous rainy‑period irrigation

Remove and burn crop residues after harvest

Groundnut - Rust (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves getting small brown or orange pustules?

Why are leaves drying quickly?

Symptoms:

Small raised brown or orange‑brown pustules on leaf upper and lower surface

Pustules may break open, leaves turn yellow and dry early

Plants look weak, yield reduced

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Puccinia arachidis in humid, warm weather.

Solution:

Use rust‑tolerant groundnut varieties

Spray triazole fungicides like Propiconazole or Carbendazim + Mancozeb 0.2–0.3% at early pustules

Prevention:

Avoid dense planting and excess nitrogen

Keep field drainage and spacing good

Rotate with non‑host crops and destroy infected crop debris

Groundnut - Stem Rot / Sclerotium Rot (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are plants lodging and stems rotting near soil?

Why are stems turning brown and weak?

Symptoms:

Stem near soil turns brown, soft, may girdle and snap

Plants lodge, branches may die from base upwards

White fungal growth and black sclerotia seen on affected stem

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Sclerotium rolfsii in humid, wet conditions.

Solution:

Remove and destroy infected plants early

Avoid heavy, continuous irrigation; keep soil not too wet

Use fungicides or bio‑agents like Trichoderma in severe cases as per local advisory

Prevention:

Use well‑drained soil and proper spacing

Avoid dense, nitrogen‑rich, rank growth

Rotate with non‑legume crops and plough in residues

Groundnut - Aspergillus Flavus Infection – Aflatoxin (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are kernels turning dark and musty‑smelling?

Why are some grains discoloured or bad‑tasting?

Symptoms:

Kernels dark brown to black, may show musty or earthy smell

Seeds may be shrivelled, with poor germination

In severe cases, grains unsafe for human or animal feed

Cause:
Fungal infection by Aspergillus flavus under hot, dry, and moist‑storage conditions.

Solution:

Harvest at right maturity, avoid delaying harvest in hot, dry weather

Dry pods quickly to 8–10% moisture before storage

Store in cool, dry, well‑ventilated place; avoid damp godowns

Prevention:

Avoid mechanical damage during threshing and transport

Inspect grain before storage and remove damaged or mouldy kernels

Use clean, dry gunny bags or bins; avoid mixing with other crops

Groundnut - Nutrient Deficiency – Nitrogen (Nutrient Deficiency)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are older leaves turning pale yellow?

Why is crop thin and growth slow?

Symptoms:

Older leaves pale green to yellow, starting from tip and margin

Plants thin, with less branching and slow development

Fewer flowers and pods, light‑coloured pods

Cause:
Low nitrogen in soil or poor nodulation and rhizobium activity.

Solution:

Inoculate seeds with rhizobium culture at sowing

Apply 10–15 kg/ha nitrogen at early vegetative stage if soil test is low

Include organic manure or compost to improve soil fertility

Prevention:

Follow soil test‑based fertilizer recommendation

Avoid excess nitrogen that reduces nodule formation

Use good‑quality seed and timely sowing

Groundnut - Nutrient Deficiency – Calcium (Nutrient Deficiency)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are pods empty or seeds stuck inside shell?

Why are kernels shrivelled even though shells look normal?

Symptoms:

Pods with empty or poorly filled seeds, seeds stuck to shell

Kernels shrivelled, low weight, poor germination

Plant may look normal, but yield and quality low

Cause:
Low calcium in soil or poor pod‑filling due to uneven water.

Solution:

Apply gypsum or calcium sulphate 250–400 kg/ha at flowering to pod‑filling stage

Keep soil moisture even; avoid dryness at pegging and pod‑filling

Prevention:

Avoid delaying irrigation when pods start developing

Use balanced fertilizers with calcium on sandy, low‑Ca soils

Test soil once and correct calcium status before sowing if needed

Groundnut - White Grubs (Pest)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are young plants dying suddenly in patches?

Why are roots and stems chewed and rotting?

Symptoms:

Seedlings or early plants suddenly wilt and die

Roots and underground stems gnawed, with white‑creamy grubs nearby

Plants may fall over easily when pulled

Cause:
Soil‑dwelling white grubs feeding on roots and pegs before pod formation.

Solution:

Apply granular insecticides like Chlorpyrifos or approved alternatives at sowing or early stage as per local dose

Use neem cake or other bio‑agents in organic systems where allowed

Prevention:

Plough field early to expose grubs for birds and sun

Avoid continuous groundnut in same field

Maintain good crop rotation with cereals or other non‑legumes

Groundnut - Red Hairy Caterpillar (Pest)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves and stems chewed in patches?

Why are plants skeletonized and weak?

Symptoms:

Leaves and stems shredded, eaten in patches

Young plants may be completely defoliated

Hairy reddish‑brown caterpillars seen on plants at night

Cause:
Caterpillar feeding on leaves and stems in early to mid‑crop stage.

Solution:

Spray Carbaryl or Quinalphos 0.05–0.08% at early damage, repeat 1–2 times at 7–10‑day intervals if heavy

Hand‑pick and destroy caterpillars in small fields

Prevention:

Maintain field sanitation, destroy alternate weed hosts

Plough field and expose pupae before sowing

Avoid dense, rank growth that shelters pests
