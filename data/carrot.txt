=== CROP: Carrot ===

Carrot - Leaf Blight – Cercospora / Alternaria (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves getting brown or circular patches?

Why are leaves drying from bottom upwards?

Symptoms:

Small brown or dark spots on older leaves, with dark margin

Spots may join, leaves turn yellow and drop early

Reduced leaf area, roots smaller and light‑coloured

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Cercospora or Alternaria in humid, wet conditions.

Solution:

Spray Mancozeb or Chlorothalonil 0.2–0.3% at first spots, repeat 2–3 times at 10‑day intervals

Avoid spraying during strong mid‑day sun

Prevention:

Keep spacing open for good air movement

Avoid overhead irrigation on leaves

Remove infected leaves and crop residues after harvest

Carrot - Black Root Rot / Pythium Root Rot (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are roots turning black and soft?

Why are plants thin and leaves yellowing?

Symptoms:

Root tips or parts of taproot turn black and soft

Roots may be stubby, plants slow‑growing and yellow

Severe cases show damping‑off‑like death of seedlings

Cause:
Soil‑borne fungi like Pythium or other root‑rot pathogens in wet, heavy soil.

Solution:

Avoid heavy, water‑logged soil; use raised beds

Use well‑drained soil and avoid over‑irrigation

In severe cases, treat seed or seedbed with approved fungicides

Prevention:

Treat seed with fungicides before sowing

Avoid continuous carrot in same bed

Incorporate organic matter to improve soil structure

Carrot - Cavity Spot (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are roots showing deep, dark cavities?

Why are outer skin cracked and rotten inside?

Symptoms:

Dark, sunken, irregular cavities or pits on root surface

Cavities may be small or deep, roots lose weight and market value

Plants may look normal but roots are damaged

Cause:
Fungal or complex soil‑borne disease in heavy, overly wet soils.

Solution:

Avoid heavy, water‑logged soil and excessive irrigation

Grow in light, sandy loam soil with good drainage

Rotate with non‑root crops and reduce continuous carrot

Prevention:

Do not grow carrot repeatedly in same field

Avoid deep tillage damage and mechanical injury to roots

Use clean seed and proper spacing

Carrot - Powdery Mildew (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves getting white powdery coating?

Why are leaves curling and drying?

Symptoms:

White, powdery fungal growth on upper leaf surface

Leaves may curl, twist and yellow early

Reduced photosynthesis, roots smaller and lighter

Cause:
Fungal disease favoured by warm days and cool, dry nights with high humidity on leaves.

Solution:

Spray Sulphur or approved fungicides 0.2–0.3% at first powdery patches

Repeat 1–2 times at 10‑day intervals if needed

Prevention:

Keep spacing open for airflow and reduce canopy density

Avoid overcrowding and excessive nitrogen

Remove heavily infected leaves if feasible

Carrot - Sclerotinia Rot / White Rot (Disease)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are roots soft and covered with white fungus?

Why are roots rotting in storage?

Symptoms:

Root becomes soft, watery, with fluffy white fungal growth

Black hard sclerotia may develop on rotting parts

Rot may start in field or continue in storage

Cause:
Fungal disease caused by Sclerotinia or similar fungi in wet, cool conditions.

Solution:

Remove and destroy infected roots and surrounding soil

Keep field and storage dry and well‑ventilated

Avoid heavy, damp soil and over‑irrigation

Prevention:

Rotate with non‑host crops like cereals

Avoid continuous carrot in same field

Store only clean, dry, undamaged roots

Carrot - Nitrogen Deficiency (Nutrient Deficiency)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are older leaves turning pale yellow?

Why is crop thin and roots weak?

Symptoms:

Older leaves pale green to yellow, starting from tip and margin

Plants thin, less foliage, slow growth

Roots small, light‑coloured, low yield

Cause:
Low nitrogen in soil or poor fertilizer management.

Solution:

Apply urea 15–25 kg/ha in split doses at early vegetative and root‑bulking stages

Combine with organic manure or compost if soil is poor

Prevention:

Follow soil test‑based NPK recommendations for carrot

Avoid heavy, late nitrogen that promotes tops over roots

Use balanced fertilizers with micronutrients on sandy soils

Carrot - Potassium Deficiency (Nutrient Deficiency)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaf tips and edges drying out?

Why are roots tough and fibrous?

Symptoms:

Leaf tips and margins turn brown, like burn

Older leaves show yellowing along edges

Roots may be tough, woody, less smooth

Cause:
Low potassium in soil, especially in light, sandy soils.

Solution:

Apply MOP 15–25 kg/ha split with basal or at early stages

Use organic manure to improve potassium retention

Prevention:

Use balanced NPK, avoid only nitrogen‑rich practice

Avoid continuous carrot without manure or K

Maintain good drainage and avoid waterlogging

Carrot - Aphids (Pest)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are leaves sticky and covered with black sooty mould?

Why are plants yellowing and roots small?

Symptoms:

Small soft insects clustered on leaf undersides

Leaves sticky from honeydew, with black sooty mould

Plants may be stunted, with reduced growth

Cause:
Aphids sucking sap and sometimes transmitting virus diseases.

Solution:

Spray Imidacloprid or Acetamiprid as per label dose at first appearance

Repeat 1–2 times at 7–10‑day intervals under heavy pressure

Prevention:

Avoid heavy nitrogen and dense planting

Maintain field sanitation and remove nearby weeds

Use yellow sticky traps to monitor aphids

Carrot - Carrot Rust Fly / Stem Maggot (Pest)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are roots tunnelled and rotten?

Why are seedlings wilting and stems damaged?

Symptoms:

Small creamy‑white maggots boring into roots and base of stem

Roots show tunnels, blackened, rotten patches

Seedlings may wilt or die from base damage

Cause:
Fly larvae feeding inside roots and lower stems.

Solution:

Avoid sowing too early; time planting to avoid peak fly activity

Use insecticide‑treated seed or soil drench as per local recommendation

Remove and destroy damaged plants and roots

Prevention:

Rotate carrot with non‑host crops

Avoid continuous carrot in same field

Keep field sanitation and destroy plant debris

Carrot - Cutworms (Pest)

Common Farmer Questions:

Why are seedlings cut at ground level?

Why are young plants falling and rotting near soil?

Symptoms:

Seedlings or young plants cut at base, lie down

Presence of fat, grayish caterpillars in soil at night

Patches of dead or missing plants in row

Cause:
Soil‑dwelling cutworm larvae feeding at night.

Solution:

Apply granular insecticides like Chlorpyrifos at sowing or early stage as per label

Use bait or hand‑picking at night in small fields

Prevention:

Plough and harrow field early to expose larvae to sun and birds

Avoid heavy, undisturbed soil before sowing

Maintain good field hygiene and avoid dense weed growth
